Every Stage in Nevada County: A Theatre Guide

Six theatre companies. Thirty performance spaces. A town of 3,000 with more stages than most cities.

Nevada City has a population of roughly 3,000 people. It also has more working theatre stages than most mid-sized cities. Walk the old downtown blocks on any given Friday night and you might pass three different productions running simultaneously in buildings separated by a few hundred feet of boardwalk. That ratio of seats to souls says something about what this community values.

"Are people coming back to the theatre post-Covid?" Jeannie Wood asked over coffee on a cool Monday morning. The answer, she decided, was yes. People want to be out and about again. And in Nevada County, they have somewhere worth going.

Community Asian Theatre of the Sierra (CATS)

CATS has been telling cultural stories on Nevada County stages for over thirty years. Founded to bring Asian American narratives to Northern California audiences, the company goes well beyond the stage itself. "Theatre is just one of the tools we use to tell our cultural stories," says Wood, the company's Executive Director and a recent Lifetime Achievement Awardee from the Sacramento Area Regional Theatre Alliance. "We tailor our workshops and field trips so that, if we present a show about the internment of Japanese Americans during World War II, we will visit Manzanar."

The breadth of CATS' programming is what sets it apart. The company collaborates with schools, partners with other theatre groups, and runs workshops that introduce theatre and Asian American culture to new generations. "We love working collaboratively with new partners to further our mission of diversity through the arts," Wood says.

Sierra Theatre Company

Michele Nesbit, Sierra Theatre Company's Executive Director, has an expansive vision. "Our goal is to offer training and experience in all aspects of the theatre, not just acting," she says. "It could be tech, promotion—anything that anyone wants to get involved in, we want to provide that."

The company runs a packed schedule: readers' theatre, musical productions, children's workshops, and fundraisers throughout the year. Their emphasis on long-term sustainability keeps standards high without burning out. And they don't work in isolation.

"Theatre is what makes us human. What it offers in terms of confidence, presence, and our place in the world just can't be beaten. There isn't a thing you can do in theatre that doesn't benefit you."

Michele Nesbit, Sierra Theatre Company

"We enjoy collaborating with other theatre groups and establishing partnerships, like the ones with CATS and Legacy Theatre," Nesbit says. In a town this small, competition between companies is beside the point. The audience is the same audience. The goal is the same goal.

Lyric Rose Theatre

Judy Merrick founded Lyric Rose Theatre with a clear-eyed mission: telling stories that challenge audiences to see each other more fully. "Theatre makes you a better person, whether you're in it, watching it, or supporting it," she says.

The company's focus on LGBTQ+ narratives and human rights stories carries a particular urgency right now. Merrick doesn't shy from that. "When LGBTQ+ rights and human rights are under attack, telling these stories on the main stage isn't just art, it's vital," she says. "We believe that when people see each other fully and compassionately, without judgment or conditioned hatred, the world changes."

"If we see one change of heart in an audience as a result of a play, we've done our job."

Judy Merrick, Lyric Rose Theatre

Through school partnerships and challenging productions, Lyric Rose lives up to that mission season after season.

LeGacy Presents

LeGacy Presents rounds out the collaborative theatre ecosystem that Sierra Theatre Company and CATS frequently partner with. The company brings musical theatre and family-friendly productions to the area's historic stages, adding another layer to a scene that somehow keeps growing despite the town's modest population.

Sierra Stages

Sierra Stages has been producing contemporary drama and world premieres in Nevada City for years, drawing on the deep pool of local acting talent and the community's appetite for work that takes risks. Their seasons tend toward plays that provoke conversation—exactly the kind of programming that thrives in a town where the audience sticks around to talk after the curtain falls.

You can see live theatre here every weekend, year-round. In a county of 100,000 people—spread across mountain ridges, river canyons, and two small downtown cores—that is remarkable. It's also, if you ask the people who make it happen, completely normal. This is just what they do here.
