New Gold: Nevada County's Wine Country You Didn't Know About

Gold miners dug the earth. Today's winemakers learned to listen to it.

Share X FB

When gold miners stormed the Sierra Nevada Foothills, they brought picks, shovels, and pans—and they brought grape vines. A lot of grape vines. As gold was pulled from the earth, vines were planted. By the 1860s, the region produced roughly 20,000 gallons of wine annually, which likely didn't enhance gold production, but did help ease the pain of an unsuccessful day.

Prohibition slowed the wine industry to a trickle, while gold mining came to a complete stop in 1957. But, unlike mining, the Foothills wine industry has made a successful comeback. By 1978, the Sierra Foothills gained its official American Viticultural Area (AVA), and is now home to twelve public wineries, all with different approaches to farming, varietals, and blends.

"Today, the North Sierra Foothills is probably the last good quality wine grape growing region in California where the average aspiring farmer can afford to give it a go. It doesn't cost a small fortune to grow wine here, which allows for a level of adventurism that you just can't find anywhere else."

— Mark Henry, Montoliva Winery

The Wineries

Szabo Vineyards

Alex Szabo searched all over California to find just the right soil for his vineyard. He found it in the Northern Sierra Foothills: extremely rocky, well-draining, and slightly acidic. The mountain effect in this region is similar to the Napa Valley, with cool evening breezes emanating from the western slopes of the Sierras. These cool breezes, combined with the soil, make for exceptionally high-quality and intensely flavored fruit.

Lucchesi Vineyards & Winery

Family-owned Lucchesi Vineyards has been producing wine on its steeply terraced, 20-acre property nestled in the Sierra Foothills for 25 years. Their handcrafted wines can be sampled at the winery with a reservation, and any time at their tasting room in the heart of downtown Grass Valley.

Naggiar Vineyards & Winery

After admiring Naggiar's undulating vineyards, scenic lawn, and pond, stay for the music. Popular cover bands perform at the winery every weekend from April through October. With unique Italian grapes and Hungarian-style blends, this South County winery is a destination unto itself.

Naggiar Vineyards & Winery

Grass Valley · Live music weekends, Italian and Hungarian varietals

See on Map →

Avanguardia Wines

Rob Chrisman's winery—Italian for "avant-garde"—plays out the region's experimental spirit. Avanguardia produces cutting-edge wines that express a variety of styles, from his Ampio red blend to his signature white, Cristallo. It's the kind of winemaking that can only happen where the cost of entry is low enough to take real risks.

Avanguardia Wines

Nevada City Winery

The founder grew up in Nevada City, traveled and studied in Europe, and returned convinced that Nevada County offered the soils and climate to make wines that challenge the world's best. One of the very first established wineries in the county, Nevada City Winery is now women-owned and operated.

"Why do we make wine? For the pure joy of the craft."

— Nevada City Winery

More to Explore

At Gray Pine, all of Guy Lauterbach's wines are from estate-grown grapes: Cabernet Sauvignon, Cabernet Franc, and Petit Verdot. At Truckee River Winery, their Sierra Foothills Sauvignon Blanc "perfectly captures the vibrant, crisp freshness of our local terroir." And Montoliva's Mark Henry proves that adventurism and affordability can coexist in the same bottle.

A Quiet Revolution: The Natural Winemakers

In the Sierra Foothills, a few winemakers are rewriting the rules. Their work happens far from polished tasting rooms and event venues, in sheds and cellars where the process begins with listening to the soil.

Arquils Wines

In Penn Valley, Carlos Caruncho farms and ferments without chemicals, additives, or manipulation. Born in Cuba, Caruncho is one of only a handful of natural winemakers in Nevada County. His wines—alive, complex, and unfiltered—speak of soil and season, not industry.

"When you work this way, you feel the land breathe."

— Carlos Caruncho, Arquils Wines

Under the Table Wines

Michelle Bostaph and Daniel Shmelkin work the land off Red Dog Road, producing honest wines with minimal intervention under their label Under the Table Wines.

Everwild Wines

In Rough and Ready, Talia and Austin have revived the Old Coufos Vineyard under the label Everwild, proving that old vines and new ideas can share the same rows.

Everwild Wines

Zumo Wine

Diego Perez, working under the label Zumo Wine, experiments quietly with local fruit while raising a young family. Together with the other natural winemakers, they form a small but growing constellation, trading ideas, tasting one another's vintages, and proving that honest, biodiverse farming can yield wines of remarkable vitality.

For now, these bottles are hard to find locally—though Arquils appears on menus at The Stone House and Lola's at The National Exchange Hotel in Nevada City, and at Tahoe Wine Collective in Truckee. Yet their influence is spreading as a growing movement.

In a region once known for mining the earth, this new generation of vintners is learning instead to listen to it.
