Sound Check

Every venue. Every night. One list.

Share X FB

Nevada County's music scene punches above its weight. Way above. This is a rural foothill community that has produced a harpist who became one of the most respected songwriters of her generation, a drummer who cofounded Death Grips, and a guitarist who joined The Shins. The same network of basement shows, grange halls, and scrappy all-ages venues that launched those careers is still running—still producing new artists, still booking shows every night of the week. From punk basements to concert halls, here's how that happened and where to find it.

Lucky Little Towns: A 40-Year Music History

Those of us playing in rock bands in Western Nevada County in the mid-to-late '90s knew we were lucky, and that we owed a debt to the punk bands who'd played here in the decade before. We'd grown up going to see The Circus Tents, Blowhole, and Doppelgänger playing at Seaman's Lodge, the Rough & Ready Grange, and almost constant house parties.

Legendarily, a 1992 Circus Tents set at the Nevada Union Car Show descended into chaos, and immediately afterwards, the band drove to Bear River High School to compete in a Battle of the Bands, which also descended into chaos. They won. Being too young to attend, events like these became folklore among my cohort. Later, when a Circus Tents sticker appeared in a Green Day music video, it felt like our scene had made it. It didn't take much to get us excited in 1994.

"Those of us playing in rock bands in Western Nevada County in the mid-to-late '90s knew we were lucky, and that we owed a debt to the punk bands who'd played here in the decade before."

Adam Kline, Mayor of Nevada City

The next wave took cues from the styles emerging at the time: grunge, pop-punk, and ska. In 1995, visionary Nevada Union High School teacher Loren Brown founded NU Unplugged, an annual night of student music at the Don Baggett Theatre, and the community supported it. A quartet of talented teens calling themselves Chode performed to acclaim. Despite their youth, they played credible grunge music, and if you were playing in another local band at the time, you knew they were irritatingly good and well-rehearsed. Their counterpoint was a quintet called Badical Turbo Radness—a fun, goofy party band.

For these bands and their contemporaries, Circus Tents frontman Matt Wedgley was an unlikely respected elder statesman. Eventually, Chode released a cassette, BTR released a CD, and while both were locally popular, both groups split up shortly thereafter.

The Breakout Generation

What happened next is where the story gets improbable. Hunter, BTR's drummer, left to join the celebrated punk band A.F.I., leaving other members to start a British Invasion-styled trio called the Gears. Members of Chode formed Pocket For Corduroy, a more focused, Fugazi-influenced incarnation. For listeners wanting something more challenging, there was Legs on Earth: a modern New Wave/prog quartet led by powerhouse Sacramento drummer Zach Hill, with his cousin and two local fellas.

The short life of a group called In Memory of Radio led to their frontman establishing himself as one of the finest regional singer-songwriters: Aaron Ross. Harpist Joanna Newsom, while a fan of groups like Chode and PFC—both featuring her brother Peter on drums—and the Gears, instead drew creative inspiration from local-but-globally-esteemed minimalist composer Terry Riley. Joanna's early fascination with her instrument, facility with language, and time spent with local music teacher Lisa Stine led her to a style uniquely her own.

Best friends since childhood, Alela Diane and Mariee Sioux were mainstays in the audience for shows by the Gears, PFC, and especially Aaron Ross. His songwriting and folky, acoustic sound were particularly inspirational to the two of them as they began writing songs themselves, booking shows in much-loved local spots like the Magic Theatre (now The Onyx), St. Joseph's Hall, and Pioneer Park.

"As Joanna's career took off, Alela and Mariee saw that, with work, they might have a similar trajectory. So they got to work."

Adam Kline

Legs on Earth, the Gears, and PFC gave way to Hella, Golden Shoulders, and Them Hills, respectively, and great bands like Black Bear, Casual Fog, Pisces Knife, the Pleased, and countless others formed around this time. Joanna Newsom was being embraced internationally, and with the 2006–2010 reign of a local label, Grass Roots Record Company, the feeling in town was that we had a proper music scene happening. People living elsewhere started noticing something was going on here.

Where They Are Now

Joanna is now one of the most respected songwriters of her generation, still recording and performing. Alela and Mariee have been prolific, releasing quality albums and touring worldwide. Hunter is still with A.F.I. and playing to massive audiences. Jon from the Circus Tents is drumming for The Shins—one of the most acclaimed indie acts in the U.S. And Zach Hill is known as the mastermind behind Death Grips.

Locally, Aaron Ross and Golden Shoulders remain active and appreciated, while high-energy bands like Beautiful Dudes, Checked-Out, and Earth Exit are carrying the banner first raised by the Circus Tents. Western Nevada County's tradition of exceptional singer-songwriters carries on with Davia, Graham Farrow, Brett Shady, and Two Runner.

"We're still lucky."

Adam Kline
