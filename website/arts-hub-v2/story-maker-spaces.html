Make Something: Maker Spaces & Craft Studios

A blacksmithing class before lunch. Ceramics after.

Share X FB

Most places talk about the maker movement in the abstract. Nevada County just does it. Within a twenty-minute drive of downtown Grass Valley, you can learn to weld sculpture from salvage metal, throw pots in a wood-fired kiln, build furniture from reclaimed timber, or forge iron at an anvil. The density of working studios, shared workshops, and open-door maker spaces here is genuinely unusual for a rural county. It's not a trend. It's been the culture for decades.

What follows is a guide to the places where you can walk in, pick up a tool, and make something with your hands.

Overall Collective Design

"Living, working, manufacturing, building—we are doing everything I love here," says welder and artist Sally Peterson. She's standing on the 28-acre makers compound between Nevada City and Grass Valley that she and her husband Dave, both former teachers, purchased as a mule ranch years ago with vague plans for a hobby farm.

Things evolved. Today, Peterson shares the property with her partners at Overall Collective Design: woodworker Monica Hughes and designer Patti Glenn. The outbuildings house Peterson's fabrication studio, Hughes' cabinetry shop, and Glenn's jewelry soldering bench. There's a large kiln for pottery, an extensive tool shed, and a separate building for the Burning Man installations they've hauled to the playa over the years.

"The way I approach interior design is similar to how I approach my art, through intriguing found objects. Everything needs to have soul, personality, and a heartbeat."

Sally Peterson, Overall Collective Design

Peterson wasn't always a welder, though she always considered herself artistic. While teaching art and PE, she took a welding class at the Curious Forge and started making hand-fabricated metal sculptures from found objects and salvage metal. Her home became a work of art. Oprah—yes, that Oprah—took notice and featured Peterson's dumpster-diving-inspired work.

Hughes, meanwhile, rekindled a passion for custom woodworking she'd carried since growing up in her father's cabinet shop in Boulder, Colorado. "It took me a while to call myself an artist," Hughes says. "I thought I was just making cabinets."

In 2019, Peterson and Hughes officially became Overall Collective Design, collaborating on projects for businesses in Nevada City, Grass Valley, Truckee, Lake Tahoe, Reno, San Francisco, New Mexico, and Hawaii. Their portfolio includes one-of-a-kind interior designs for Courtyard Suites, Elevation 2477, Telestream, Miners Foundry Cultural Center, the Stone House, Pete's Pizza, and the Center for the Arts.

"Our aesthetic is modern industrial, but when you use reclaimed materials, it brings a whole different feel to an object or space. Everyone deserves to live or work in a beautiful space."

Monica Hughes & Sally Peterson, Overall Collective Design

The Curious Forge

After fateful trips to Burning Man and a Maker Faire in San Mateo in 2011, Liam Ellerby and Kara Asilanis founded The Curious Forge on Bitney Springs Road in Nevada City. Their intent: cultivate an environment that fosters innovation, collaboration, and learning.

With over 20,000 square feet of equipment, tools, and studio space, the Curious Forge is the region's anchor makerspace. The operation offers memberships for independent makers, plus a rotating slate of art classes, workshops, and workforce development programs. Walk through on any given afternoon and you'll hear the crack of a welding arc, the whine of a bandsaw, and the steady tap of a metalsmith's hammer.

It was a Curious Forge welding class that launched Sally Peterson's career as a sculptor. That kind of chain reaction—one class leading to a new life direction—is the whole point.

Indian Springs Art & Ceramic Center

Tucked along Indian Springs Road in Penn Valley, ISACC is a project art space and gallery devoted to the ceramic arts. Founded by ceramicist Glenn Husted, the center's mission is to foster public appreciation of the clay story—from raw earth to finished form—and to create awareness of the unique contemporary ceramic work happening in the foothills.

The center hosts classes, exhibitions, and open studio days. For anyone who has ever wanted to feel wet clay on a wheel but didn't know where to start, this is the place.

Truckee Roundhouse

On the eastern side of the county, the Truckee Roundhouse serves the mountain community with a community makerspace model similar to the Curious Forge. Equipped with woodworking tools, a metal shop, laser cutters, 3D printers, and textile equipment, the Roundhouse runs classes for all skill levels and provides open shop hours for members. It's proof that the maker ethos isn't confined to the western foothills—it extends to the high Sierra as well.

North Columbia Schoolhouse Cultural Center

Twenty minutes outside Nevada City on the San Juan Ridge sits a one-room schoolhouse built in 1875 to educate miners' children. Since 1982, the North Columbia Schoolhouse Cultural Center has served as the Ridge's gathering place—part folk art venue, part community hall, part eclectic cultural anchor.

The Schoolhouse's programming runs from craft fairs to concerts to the acclaimed Sierra Storytelling Festival. Early offerings included classical Indian dance classes, power tool workshops for women, and didgeridoo performances. The spirit hasn't changed. It's still the kind of place where a 15-year embroidery project involving 243 people of all ages can come to life—which is exactly what happened with the San Juan Ridge Tapestry Project, conceptualized by local weaver Marsha Stone.

"If you want to get together with someone, and it's not someone's house or a bar, then you go there. It's like the homeplace."

Nancy Lorenz, Schoolhouse board member

You don't need a degree or a portfolio to walk into any of these spaces. You just need to show up and be willing to try something. Bring gloves. Expect sparks. Leave with something you made with your own hands.
