A field guide to our cultural corridors

Our Cultural Corridors

In the heart of the Sierra Nevada, Nevada County’s highways are more than just roads — they are cultural corridors. Highways 49, 20, and 40, each with its own distinct heritage, weave together the stories of California’s First Peoples, Gold Rush miners, artists, and dreamers — connecting the county’s California Cultural Districts, from Truckee to Grass Valley–Nevada City.

01

Highway 49

The golden chain of history

Running north to south through Nevada County, Highway 49 — named for the “49ers” who rushed into California’s goldfields — is famously known as the Golden Chain Highway, linking the historic Gold Rush towns that once defined California’s economy. Travelers today can explore Empire Mine State Historic Park in Grass Valley, one of the oldest, deepest, and richest gold mines in California.

Further north, the San Juan Ridge unfolds in towns like North San Juan and the preserved ghost town of North Bloomfield at Malakoff Diggins State Historic Park. On Tyler Foote Road stands the North Columbia Schoolhouse Cultural Center, an 1875 schoolhouse built by miners for their children — now home to artists, film screenings, poetry readings, and the internationally renowned Sierra Storytelling Festival.

02

Highway 20

From wagon trail to modern artery

Stretching east to west across Nevada County, Highway 20 traces its roots to the 19th-century California Trail, used by emigrants in wagons seeking new lives in California. What began as a rugged pioneer road evolved into one of the state’s most scenic drives. Just off the highway, the Little Town of Washington flanks the South Yuba River, where the historic Washington Hotel, built in 1857, still serves as the community hub and rallying point for the town’s Fourth of July Parade.

Further south is Rough and Ready, founded in 1849, which proudly recalls its three-month rebellion as the Great Republic of Rough and Ready each June during Secession Days — its mining heritage alive in street names like Ironclad, Gamble Court, and the unforgettable To Hell and Back Lane.

03

Highway 40

The Lincoln Highway and the road to the west

Before Interstate 80 sped travelers through the Sierra, there was “Old Highway 40” — part of America’s first transcontinental road, the legendary Lincoln Highway, stretching from Times Square to San Francisco. Here, every curve tells a story: ancient petroglyphs near Donner Summit, etched into granite by the ancestors of today’s Washoe People, and Van Norden Meadow, where the South Fork of the Yuba River begins its journey to the foothills.

Clair Tappaan Lodge, built in the 1930s by Sierra Club volunteers, still welcomes adventurers; Rainbow Lodge, a 1920s retreat of native stone and timber, recalls the golden age of alpine travel. And from Donner Lake to Cisco Grove, the “20 Mile Museum” tells the evolving story of migration and innovation through a series of interpretive signs.

The Living Part

The corridor keeps changing — so does the map

A studio opens. A trail gets re-routed. A festival moves to a new lot. No single editor can keep up with a whole county — which is why the guide is built to be added to.

If you run a venue, lead a walk, or simply know a place the map is missing, you can put it on the map. Every contribution is one more honest dot in the field guide.
