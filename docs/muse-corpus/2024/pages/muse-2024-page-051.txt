Eliza Tudor, who directs the festival, speaks of its
approachable nature: “If ever there were evidence that
poetry can be brought in from the margins, our festival is it.
From pop-up readings to open mics, and poetic cinema and
poetry in the park; to ‘pay with a poem’ initiatives in coffee
shops and evening collaborations with pubs and galleries,
it all kicks off on World Poetry Day in late March.”
Past presenters at Sierra Poetry Festival’s mainstage event
have included poets laureate from across the state such as
Robin Coste Lewis, Lee Herrick, Kim Shuck, Indigo Moor and
the late Al Young, as well as Pulitzer Prize-winning Forrest
Gander and Nevada County’s own Gary Snyder. Marcelo
Hernandez Castillo, Rooja Mohassessy, Maxima Kahn,
Karen Terrey, Judy Crowe, Molly Fisk and Doc Dachtler are
among many others who have presented from closer to
home. Last year’s festival, with its theme of Be-longing,
brought poets from Australia together in conversation
with Sands Hall, author, playwright, musician and teacher.
Mark Tredinnick, and Aboriginal poets Jennifer Kemarre
Martiniello and Judith Nangala Crispin, discussed agency
and sovereignty in poetry, and the importance of place.

 Each year, the selected theme for Sierra
 Poetry Festival grapples with the truths of
 the moment and the climate of our times.
“This year’s theme is Storm and Sanctuary,”
 explains Hall, a member of the Nevada County
 Arts Council’s literary committee, “and the
 committee tosses around a lot of options
 for themes each year. A good theme is broad
 enough that our poets f ind resonance with
 it in their own work, yet is specif ic and
 concrete—as, of course, all good writing is.”
