## 2. Audience Segments / Personas

Four priority audiences are defined (Page 10):

### A. Arts and Culture Enthusiasts
- Locals and visitors, NCAC members, arts organizations' audiences
- **Sub-audience: local art enthusiasts**
- **Sub-audience: visitors**

### B. Artists, Culture Bearers, and Arts and Cultural Organizations
- Artists, arts administrators, arts advocates, creatives, Indigenous artists, culturally specific arts organizations

### C. Funders
- Administrators, officials, and board members of agencies funding arts and culture (government and private); individual donors; decision-makers

### D. Partners
- State, County, and City/Town officials, non-profit organizations in other sectors (recreation, health, tourism, education), small businesses, tribal government representatives, culturally specific non-profit organizations
- Explicitly excludes funding organizations

**Note:** The doc acknowledges these audiences overlap (e.g., individual donors may be arts enthusiasts).

### Underserved populations specifically called out:
- Tribal communities (Nisenan, Tsi Akim Maidu, Washoe Tribes)
- Latino/a/x populations
- Youth
- Black residents and People of Color
- Immigrant communities

## 3. Messaging Principles

Six messaging principles (Page 13):

1. **Inclusive** -- Tailor messaging to diverse audience needs, especially youth and underrepresented groups
2. **Inspiring and Community-Centered** -- Highlight local impact and engagement, moving beyond transactional
3. **Accessible and Integrated** -- Promote arts as fun, accessible, woven into daily life (like sports or nature)
4. **Affordable and Reciprocal** -- Reflect affordability and community-based values
5. **Authentic and Trust-Building** -- Prioritize ongoing communication to foster trust, reduce scarcity mindsets, encourage collaboration
6. **Value-Oriented** -- Position arts as vital infrastructure contributing economic and social impact, justifying public investment

### Messaging creation formula (Page 14):
> What + Why + Who + How + Call to Action = Key Message

### Responsible Tourism principles (Page 42):
1. Shop local
2. Recreate responsibly
3. Be part of the community and volunteer
4. Plan an offseason trip

## 4. Three Flagship Projects

### Project 1: Cultural Seasons Initiative
- Year-round calendar of themed cultural programming
- Tied to Goal 1 (unified communications narrative promoting year-round activities)
- Curate a year-long calendar of targeted events and promote through multi-channel marketing campaigns

### Project 2: Creative Network Exchange
- The digital hub itself (Goal 2)
- Unifies arts calendar + Cultural Asset Map
- Includes: artist networking/forum, resource center, professional development, funder information portal
- Bilingual platform
- Directory of artists and creative communities

### Project 3: "Arts Move Mountains" Campaign
- County-wide marketing campaign (Goal 3)
- Amplify significance/visibility of arts and culture
- Stimulate economic development
- Build positive brand image around "mountain communities" identity
- Physical kiosks at key entry points with real-time, multilingual maps and event info
- Regional scope: Sacramento, Bay Area

## 6. SWOT Analysis Highlights Relevant to Digital Platform

### Strengths
- $66 million in total economic activity, 1,300+ jobs
- 74.8% of non-local visitors cite arts/culture as primary reason for visiting
- Existing resources: Cultural Asset Map, MUSE magazine, calls to artists, newsletters, community arts calendar, creatives meetups, artist and business directories, jobs board, membership program
- High level of community engagement in the arts
- Growing reputation of NCAC and its two California Cultural Districts
- Strong recognition of the value of arts and creative industries

### Weaknesses
- **Lack of unified brand architecture** among Cultural District partners -- largely due to underfunding and infrastructure gaps
- **"Start-up mindset"** in marketing -- short-term actions rather than unified strategy
- **Communication gaps and limited event visibility** -- the core problem the hub solves
- **Underrepresentation** of diverse communities in marketing
- Overall capacity and budget limitations
- Underfunded and disparate countywide communication infrastructure
- **Desire for a centralized calendar and shared digital platforms / hub**
- Challenge balancing cultural tourism messaging with meaningful access for full-time residents

### Opportunities
- Unify arts/culture coalition for collaborative communication
- **Developing a centralized digital hub** leveraging existing Cultural Asset Map and calendar
- Integrating arts into broader County/City/Town plans
- Leveraging community ties through "personal invites"
- Advocating for arts as "essential infrastructure"

### Threats
- Potential shifts in state/local policies from federal funding changes
- Persistent exclusion of diverse communities
- Limited audiences in rural areas
- Fragmented opportunities for youth
- "Not in my neighborhood" sentiment hindering new cultural spaces
- General low local participation alongside increased tourism -- requires careful management

## 7. Content Categories, Experience Types, and Event Taxonomies

### Audience Experiential Dimensions (what resonates)
Three key experiential themes identified through community engagement (Page 10):

1. **Community and Identity** -- Support local artists/culture bearers, foster sense of place, intimate/niche/community-specific programming, inclusive experiences

## 9. Evaluation Metrics They Care About

### Quantitative KPIs

**Community Participation:**
- Total attendance/participation (residents + visitors) via Placer.AI
- Engagement from underserved communities (percentage/count from Latino/a/x, Indigenous, Youth)
- Number of free/affordable events
- Attendance at cultural heritage sites

**Collaboration and Advocacy:**
- Number of active Cultural Ambassadors
- Total volunteer hours
- New strategic partnerships (cross-sector: recreation, health, tourism, environment, education)

**Economic:**
- Jobs supported by arts/culture sector

**Digital (for the hub specifically):**
- Website traffic: visits, page views, bounce rates (Google Analytics, Adobe Analytics, Squarespace)
- Digital Hub unique visitors/sessions (monthly)
- Event page views vs. overall site views (percentage directed to event listings)
- Campaign reach and impressions
- Social media engagement: likes, shares, comments, follower growth
- Newsletter: open rates, CTR to event pages, conversion rate to ticket sales/registrations, subscriber growth/churn, email sharing/forwarding
- Media coverage: mentions in news, blogs, social media

**Brand / Sentiment:**
- Net Promoter Score (NPS) -- likelihood of recommending Nevada County cultural experiences
- Brand sentiment score -- positive/negative mentions
- Message consistency score -- percentage of partner communications aligned with Culture Forward principles
- Community arts calendar submissions (month-over-month growth)

### Qualitative KPIs

**Community Participation and Engagement:**
- Percentage of respondents expressing positive/negative view of arts sector's impact
- Tools: surveys, focus groups, listening sessions

**Community Well-Being and Equity:**
- Sense of belonging, perception of fairness/equity, positive mental health, engagement/participation
- Tools: in-depth interviews, focus groups, ethnographic observations, open-ended survey questions
- Software: Google NotebookLM, SurveyMonkey, Google Forms

### OKRs tied to each goal:

| Goal | Objective | Key Result |
|------|-----------|------------|
| 1 | Achieve consistent year-round promotion across all partner channels | Increase co-marketed events by partners |
| 2 | Hub becomes primary centralized source for arts/culture events | Increase hub traffic; reduce "Lack of information" as participation barrier |
| 3 | Position arts as "essential infrastructure" and economic driver | Increase favorable perception among officials and business partners |
| 4 | Meaningful engagement from diverse/underserved communities | Increase participation from Latino/a/x, Black, Youth at sponsored events |
| 5 | Secure greater financial and policy support | Increase local/county government funding and/or policy support |
| 6 | Active base of advocates extending organizational capacity | Grow registered Cultural Ambassadors and volunteer hours |

## 12. Strategic Framework Directions

The Culture Forward Strategic Framework is organized around four strategic directions:

1. **Activation, Recreation and Engagement** -- Year-round cultural life through events, festivals, accessible creative experiences
2. **Learning, Skills and Career Pathways** -- Creative career development, mentorship, apprenticeships, CTE programs
3. **Infrastructure, Resources, and Partnerships** -- Arts as "essential infrastructure," securing investment
4. **Land, Culture and Stewardship** -- Cultural Asset Map, Indigenous perspectives, environmental connection

Three core implementation plans under the framework:
1. **Regional Marketing Plan** (this document)
2. **Funding Plan** (separate document, four-phase roadmap 2026-2031)
3. **Creative Career Pathways Plan** (separate document)
